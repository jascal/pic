Matched filter takes w = ft; the optimum sweeps every w. In symmetric tight frames they coincide — drag a phasor to make them part.

Simplex M=2, n=3

Three phasors at 120°. Every inner product is −1/2, so m_MF = 1 − (−½) = 1.5. Steering can't beat the matched filter — perfect symmetry. The margin exceeds 1 precisely because the coherence is negative: competitors point away.

This is the 1 + 1/M case the writeup flags.

Pentagon M=2, n=5

Nearest neighbours sit at 72°, so max signed coherence is cos 72° ≈ 0.309 and m_MF ≈ 0.691. Again symmetric, so the optimum lands on the same value — the trace peaks exactly at w = f_t.

The far pairs (144°) give cos = −0.809: large magnitude coherence, but it never touches the margin, which only feels the loudest positive competitor.

When steering wins asymmetry

Break the symmetry and the trace's peak slides off f_t: tilt w away from the single worst competitor, lose a little on the target, win more on the margin. That's the green gain — the gap m_opt − m_MF > 0.

In a tight frame everyone has identical neighbours, so there's nothing to tilt toward. That's the writeup's core claim made visible.

Welch floor (M=2): μ ≥ √((n−2)/(2(n−1))) — a bound on coherence magnitude; margins read signed coherence, so negatives help.   Frame operator S = Σ f_if_iᵀ = [[1.50, 0.00],[0.00, 1.50]] .   Note: in M=2 margins shrink toward 0 as n grows; the dimension-dependent floor ~1−1/√M only opens up in higher M.
