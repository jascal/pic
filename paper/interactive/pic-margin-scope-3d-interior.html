The two shadows

Drop a perpendicular from w's tip onto a feature ray; where it lands is the projection ⟨w,f_i⟩ — the bright segment from the center. The margin is the target shadow minus the rival shadow. A negative rival shadow (it points backward) helps.

Steering = swinging w

Move w and both shadows change. Tilt toward the target and its shadow grows; tilt away from the binding rival and that shadow shrinks. The optimum is the swing that maximizes the difference — and in symmetric frames it's already straight down f_t.

The rival switches

Steer w far enough and the amber ray jumps to a different competitor — you've crossed a power-diagram border into another cell. At that border two rivals tie and the margin pinches; that's what bounds how far steering can take you.

Welch floor (M=3): μ ≥ √((n−3)/(3(n−1))) · coherence magnitude.   Frame operator S = Σ f_if_iᵀ .   Small n is clearest here — the interior stays uncluttered and every shadow is easy to follow.
